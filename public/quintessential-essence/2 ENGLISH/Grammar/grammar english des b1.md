# 1 Verb Tenses

## 1.1 Present Simple

### 1.1.1 Form
- **Statement**:
  - I/you/we/they **play** ...
  - He/she/it **plays** ...
- **Negative**:
  - I/you/we/they **do not (don’t) play** ...
  - He/she/it **does not (doesn’t) play** ...
- **Question**:
  - **Do** I/you/we/they **play** ...?
  - **Does** he/she/it **play** ...?

### 1.1.2 Use
- **Present habits**: Ex: Marsha *goes* to dance lessons every Saturday.
- **Permanent situations**: Ex: *Does* Dan *work* at the cinema?
- **States**: Ex: I *like* the new James Bond film.
- **General truths**: Ex: You *play* chess with 32 pieces.

### 1.1.3 Helpful Hints
- Often used with:
  - **Adverbs**: *always*, *usually*, *often*, *sometimes*, *rarely*, *never*
  - **Phrases**: *every Monday/week/etc*, *each Monday/week/etc*, *once/twice a week/month/etc*, *three times a week/month/etc*
- Adverbs usually go **before the verb**, but **after the verb be**.
- Ex: I *always play* the piano. / I *am always* late.

## 1.2 Present Continuous

### 1.2.1 Form
- **Statement**:
  - I **am (‘m) playing** ...
  - He/she/it **is (‘s) playing** ...
  - You/we/they **are (‘re) playing** ...
- **Negative**:
  - I **am not (‘m not) playing** ...
  - He/she/it **is not (isn’t / ‘s not) playing** ...
  - You/we/they **are not (aren’t / ‘re not) playing** ...
- **Question**:
  - **Am** I **playing** ...?
  - **Is** he/she/it **playing** ...?
  - **Are** you/we/they **playing** ...?

### 1.2.2 Use
- **Actions happening now**: Ex: Jan *is watching* a DVD upstairs.
- **Temporary situations**: Ex: She *is working* at the museum until the end of the month.
- **Annoying habits** (usually with *always*): Ex: My brother *is always borrowing* my CDs without asking!

### 1.2.3 Helpful Hints
- Often used with: *now*, *right now*, *at the moment*, *today*, *this week/month/etc*

## 1.3 Past Simple

### 1.3.1 Form
- **Statement**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **played** ...
- **Negative**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **did not (didn’t) play** ...
- **Question**: **Did** I/you/he/she/it/we/they **play** ...?

### 1.3.2 Use
- **Completed actions**: Ex: I *saw* the new James Bond film yesterday.
- **Repeated actions in the past**: Ex: I *went* to the theatre four times last month.
- **General truths about the past**: Ex: Fifty years ago, people *didn’t spend* as much on entertainment as they do today.
- **Main events in a story**: Ex: Josh *pushed* the door open and *looked* inside the room.

### 1.3.3 Helpful Hints
- Often used with: *yesterday*, *last week/summer/year/etc*, *in January/2001/etc*, *an hour/a week/a year ago*

## 1.4 Past Continuous

### 1.4.1 Form
- **Statement**:
  - I/he/she/it **was playing** ...
  - You/we/they **were playing** ...
- **Negative**:
  - I/he/she/it **was not (wasn’t) playing** ...
  - You/we/they **were not (weren’t) playing** ...
- **Question**:
  - **Was** I/he/she/it **playing** ...?
  - **Were** you/we/they **playing** ...?

### 1.4.2 Use
- **Actions happening at a moment in the past**: Ex: At nine o’clock last night, I *was watching* TV.
- **Two actions in progress at the same time**: Ex: I *was reading* a book while you *were doing* the washing-up.
- **Background information in a story**: Ex: It *was raining* so Wendy *decided* to go to the cinema.

### 1.4.3 Helpful Hints
- Often used with: *at that moment*, *at one/two/etc o’clock*, *while*
- **Watch out**:
  - Use past simple and past continuous together for one action interrupting another: Ex: The phone *rang* while I *was watching* a DVD.
  - Do not use past continuous for regular or repeated actions: Ex: *Last year, I was going to the cinema every weekend.* (Wrong)

## 1.5 Present Perfect Simple

### 1.5.1 Form
- **have/has + past participle**
- **Statement**: I/you/we/they **have (‘ve) learnt** ...
- **Negative**: I/you/we/they **have not (haven’t) learnt** ...
- **Question**: **Have** I/you/we/they **learnt** ...?

### 1.5.2 Use
- **Situations that started in the past and are still true**: Ex: Mrs Jenkins *has been* the head teacher for three years.
- **Completed actions at an unspecified past time**: Ex: I *’ve already read* that book.
- **Completed actions with present results**: Ex: They *’ve all done* their homework.

### 1.5.3 Helpful Hints
- Often used with: *for*, *since*, *just*, *already*, *yet*, *ever*, *never*, *it’s the first time*
  - Ex: She *’s taught* German here *for* over five years.
  - Ex: Mr Gray *has taught* French here *since* 2006.
  - Ex: We *’ve just done* this exercise.
  - Ex: We *haven’t checked* the answers *yet*.
  - Ex: *Have* you *ever had* guitar lessons?
  - Ex: It *’s the first time* we *’ve watched* a video in class.
- **Watch out**:
  - Use past simple for specific past times: Ex: I *did* my homework last night.
  - Use present perfect for actions relevant now: Ex: I *’ve finished*! Can I go home now?

## 1.6 Present Perfect Continuous

### 1.6.1 Form
- **have/has + been + -ing**
- **Statement**:
  - I/you/we/they **have (‘ve) been studying** ...
  - He/she/it **has (‘s) been studying** ...
- **Negative**:
  - I/you/we/they **have not (haven’t) been studying** ...
  - He/she/it **has not (hasn’t) been studying** ...
- **Question**:
  - **Have** I/you/we/they **been studying** ...?
  - **Has** he/she/it **been studying** ...?

### 1.6.2 Use
- **Actions continuing up to now or just before now**: Ex: We *’ve been doing* grammar exercises for over an hour. Can we have a break now?
- **Recent actions with present effects**: Ex: They *’re having* a break now because they *’ve been working* so hard.

### 1.6.3 Helpful Hints
- Often used with: *for*, *since*, *just*
  - Ex: I *’ve been learning* English *for* over three years.
  - Ex: He *’s been learning* Chinese *since* 2004.
  - Ex: I *’ve just been reading* the school newspaper.
- **Watch out**:
  - Present perfect simple emphasizes the result: Ex: She *’s written* an article for the school newspaper. (Finished)
  - Present perfect continuous emphasizes the action and duration: Ex: She *’s been writing* an article for the school newspaper. (Not finished)

## 1.7 Past Perfect Simple

### 1.7.1 Form
- **had + past participle**
- **Statement**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **had (‘d) written** ...
- **Negative**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **had not (hadn’t) written** ...
- **Question**: **Had** I/you/he/she/it/we/they **written** ...?

### 1.7.2 Use
- **Actions and states before a past moment**: Ex: I *’d finished* my homework a few minutes before the lesson started.
- **Past results at a specific moment**: Ex: We *were* happy because we *’d all done* our homework.

### 1.7.3 Helpful Hints
- Often used with: *by*, *by the time*, *before*, *after*, *just*, *when*
  - Ex: I *’d finished* my homework *by* eight o’clock.
  - Ex: *By the time* I got to class, the lesson *had started*.
  - Ex: The teacher *had checked* the answers *before* the lesson.
  - Ex: I *left* *after* I *’d finished* the test.
- **Watch out**:
  - Past simple vs. past perfect changes meaning:
    - Ex: The lesson *started* when I *arrived*. (Arrived, then lesson started)
    - Ex: The lesson *had started* when I *arrived*. (Lesson started, then I arrived)

## 1.8 Past Perfect Continuous

### 1.8.1 Form
- **had + been + -ing**
- **Statement**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **had (‘d) been writing** ...
- **Negative**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **had not (hadn’t) been writing** ...
- **Question**: **Had** I/you/he/she/it/we/they **been writing** ...?

### 1.8.2 Use
- **Actions continuing up to or stopping just before a past moment**: Ex: We *’d been doing* grammar exercises for over an hour, so we *were* really bored!
- **Past actions with effects at a moment**: Ex: They *had* a break because they *’d been working* so hard.

### 1.8.3 Helpful Hints
- Often used with: *for*, *since*, *before*, *all day/night/etc*
  - Ex: Tony *had been studying* *for* hours, so he *had* a headache.
  - Ex: She *’d been hoping* to win the competition *since* the summer.
  - Ex: We *’d been talking* about the Internet *before* the lesson started.
- **Watch out**:
  - Past perfect simple emphasizes the result: Ex: She *’d written* an article for the school newspaper. (Finished)
  - Past perfect continuous emphasizes the action and duration: Ex: She *’d been writing* an article for the newspaper. (Not finished)

## 1.9 Future Time

### 1.9.1 Present Continuous
- **Use**: Arrangements. Ex: We *’re driving* to Berlin this weekend.
- **Watch out**:
  - Do not use for intentions: Ex: *I’m becoming an explorer when I grow up.* (Wrong)
  - Do not use for predictions: Ex: *Do you think you’re enjoying your trip to Berlin next week?* (Wrong)
  - Use *be going to* or *will* instead for intentions and predictions.

### 1.9.2 Will
- **Form**:
  - **Statement**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **will (‘ll) go** ...
  - **Negative**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **will not (won’t) go** ...
  - **Question**: ಅt Will** I/you/he/she/it/we/they **go** ...?
- **Use**:
  - Facts about the future: Ex: The new airport *will be* the biggest in Europe.
  - Predictions: Ex: You *’ll have* a great time in the Bahamas.
  - Offers and requests: Ex: We *’ll help* you get ready for your holiday.
  - Decisions made now: Ex: I know! I *’ll go* to China this summer.
- **Watch out**:
  - Use *shall* for offers/questions with I/we: Ex: *Shall* I *drive* you to the airport?
  - Do not use for arrangements: Ex: *We’ll visit my grandma this weekend.* (Wrong)

### 1.9.3 Be Going To
- **Form**:
  - **Statement**:
    - I **am (‘m) going to travel** ...
    - He/she/it **is (‘s) going to travel** ...
    - You/we/they **are (‘re) going to travel** ...
  - **Negative**:
    - I **am not (‘m not) going to travel** ...
    - He/she/it **is not (isn’t / ‘s not) going to travel** ...
    - You/we/they **are not (aren’t / ‘re not) going to travel** ...
  - **Question**:
    - **Am** I **going to travel** ...?
    - **Is** he/she/it **going to travel** ...?
    - **Are** you/we/they **going to travel** ...?
- **Use**:
  - Intentions: Ex: I *’m going to become* an explorer when I grow up.
  - Predictions (with evidence): Ex: It *’s going to rain*, so take an umbrella.
  - Facts about the future: Ex: The new airport *is going to be* the biggest in Europe.

### 1.9.4 Present Simple
- **Use**: Timetables. Ex: My plane *leaves* at six.

# 2 Prepositions of Time and Place

## 2.1 In
- **Time**:
  - Months: Ex: Paris *is* wonderful *in* April.
  - Years: Ex: I first *went* to Russia *in* 2005.
  - Seasons: Ex: We often *go* skiing *in* winter.
  - Parts of the day: Ex: My train *leaves* *in* the afternoon.
- **Place**:
  - Towns and cities: Ex: There *’s* a famous castle *in* Edinburgh.
  - Countries and continents: Ex: My brother *is* *in* Mexico.
  - Areas and regions: Ex: What *’s* life like *in* the desert?
  - Inside an object: Ex: Your passport *is* *in* the drawer.
  - Inside a room: Ex: I *’ve left* the tickets *in* the living room!
  - Inside a building: Ex: Sharon *has been* *in* the travel agent’s for an hour!
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Used in phrases: *in a minute/an hour*, *in front of*, *in the middle (of)*, *in the future*
- **Watch out**:
  - Use *to* with verbs of motion (*come*, *go*, *move*, *run*, *walk*): Ex: Was it hot when you *went to* Japan?

## 2.2 On
- **Time**:
  - Days: Ex: I *got* a new car *on* Saturday.
  - Dates: Ex: My birthday *is* *on* 19th March.
- **Place**:
  - Islands: Ex: Last year, we *stayed* *on* Mykonos.
  - Pages: Ex: There *are* some useful Italian phrases *on* page 97.
  - On top of an object: Ex: *Did* you *put* your car keys *on* the kitchen table?
  - On a surface: Ex: There *’s* a timetable *on* the wall.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Used in phrases: *on the beach*, *on the left/right*, *on my birthday*
- **Watch out**:
  - Use *in* for morning/afternoon/evening, but *on* for specific days: Ex: We *’re flying* to Washington *in* the morning / *on* Tuesday morning.
  - No preposition with *tomorrow*, *yesterday*, etc.: Ex: We *’re flying* to Washington *tomorrow afternoon*.

## 2.3 At
- **Time**:
  - Clock times: Ex: There *’s* a bus *at* ten past three.
  - Holiday periods: Ex: What *are* you *doing* *at* Christmas?
- **Place**:
  - Exact places: Ex: What *’s* it like *at* the North Pole?
  - Addresses: Ex: My cousin *lives* *at* 132 London Road.
  - Buildings (for activities): Ex: I *think* John *is* *at* the cinema, watching Titanic.
  - Activities: Ex: Rania *isn’t* here. She *’s* *at* a party.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Used in phrases: *at the moment*, *at night*, *at the top/bottom*, *at the door/window*
- **Watch out**:
  - Use *in* for larger areas, *at* for smaller places or points: Ex: We *’re spending* our next holiday *in* the countryside. Let *’s meet* *at* the train station.

# 3 Pronouns and Possessive Determiners

## 3.1 Subject Pronouns
- **Form**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they
- **Use**: The subject of a verb
- **Example**: They *built* the first aeroplane. / Alexander Fleming *discovered* penicillin, but he *did* it by mistake!

## 3.2 Object Pronouns
- **Form**: me/you/him/her/it/us/them
- **Use**: The object of a verb
- **Example**: *Could* you *give* me that equipment? / *Could* you *give* that equipment to me?

## 3.3 Possessive Determiners
- **Form**: my/your/his/her/its/our/their
- **Use**: To show who owns or has something
- **Example**: That *’s* their car. / *Is* this my coffee?
- **Watch out**: *Its* vs. *it’s*. Ex: Here *’s* the dog’s water and here *’s* its food. / It *’s* the best camera I *’ve* ever had.

## 3.4 Possessive Pronouns
- **Form**: mine/yours/his/hers/ours/theirs
- **Use**: To show who owns or has something
- **Example**: That car *is* ours.
- **Helpful Hints**: Not followed by a noun. Ex: *This is hers car.* (Wrong)
- **Watch out**: No possessive pronoun for *it*.

## 3.5 Reflexive Pronouns
- **Form**: myself/yourself/himself/herself/itself/ourselves/yourselves/themselves
- **Use**:
  - Actions where subject and object are the same: Ex: My computer *turns* itself off after half an hour.
  - To emphasize who does something: Ex: Nobody *helped* me. I *did* it myself!

## 3.6 Relative Pronouns
- **Use**: Used in relative clauses to give more information without starting a new sentence
- **Examples**:
  - *who* (people): Ex: What *’s* the name of the man *who created* the Internet?
  - *which* (things/animals): Ex: The experiment *which worked* was the last one.
  - *where* (places): Ex: This *is* the town *where* Albert Einstein *was born*.
  - *whose* (possession): Ex: That *’s* the man *whose* sister *discovered* a new planet.
- **Watch out**:
  - Use *who* for animals with personality: Ex: Our dog, *who’s called* Benji, *is* eight years old.
  - Do not repeat subject/object: Ex: *What’s the name of the man who he created the World Wide Web?* (Wrong)

#### Non-Defining Relative Clauses
- **Use**: Extra information (sentence makes sense without it)
- **Example**: That man over there, *who is called* Bill Gates, *started* Microsoft.
- **Helpful Hints**: Use commas. Ex: Carl, *whose sister is famous*, *is* a friend of mine.

#### Defining Relative Clauses
- **Use**: Essential information (sentence incomplete without it)
- **Example**: The person *who is wearing* the blue shirt *started* Microsoft.
- **Helpful Hints**: No commas. Can use *that* instead of *who*/*which*. Ex: The woman *that invented* Tippex.

# 4 Stative Verbs
- **Definition**: Describe states (feelings, thoughts), not actions. Not usually used in continuous tenses.
- **Examples**: *appear*, *be*, *believe*, *belong to*, *hate*, *have*, *include*, *know*, *like*, *love*, *need*, *prefer*, *see*, *seem*, *taste*, *think*, *understand*, *want*
- **Example**: I *like* reading books in my free time. / *I am liking reading books in my free time.* (Wrong)
- **Watch out**: Some verbs (*be*, *have*, *think*) can be used in continuous tenses for actions:
  - Ex: What *do* you *think* about his new song? / I *’m thinking* about last night’s match.

# 5 Used To
- **Form**:
  - **Statement**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **used to** ...
  - **Negative**: I/you/he/she/it/we/they **never used to** ... / **didn’t use to** ...
  - **Question**: **Did** I/you/he/she/it/we/they **use to** ...?
- **Use**: Distant past habits and states. Ex: When I *was* four, I *used to eat* ice cream every day.

# 6 The Passive

## 6.1 Present Simple, Past Simple, Will
- **Form**: *be* (in the right form) + past participle
- **Examples**:
  - **Present Simple**: Active: They *always invite* Grandma. / Passive: Grandma *is always invited*.
  - **Past Simple**: Active: They *invited* Uncle Adrian. / Passive: Uncle Adrian *was invited*.
  - **Will**: Active: They *will/won’t invite* the neighbours. / Passive: The neighbours *will/won’t be invited*.
- **Use**:
  - When we don’t know who does something: Ex: My sister’s bike *was stolen* yesterday.
  - When we don’t want/need to say who: Ex: *Was* Simon *invited*?
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Form passive from active:
    - Active: Someone *stole* my sister’s bike yesterday.
    - Passive: My sister’s bike *was stolen* yesterday.
  - For phrasal verbs, include the particle: Ex: The broken glass *was picked up*.
- **Watch out**: Some verbs have irregular past participles (see page 182).

## 6.2 Other Passive Forms
- **Form**: *be* (in the right form) + past participle
- **Examples**:
  - **Present Continuous**: Active: My aunt *is doing* the washing-up. / Passive: The washing-up *is being done* by my aunt.
  - **Present Perfect Simple**: Active: My cousin *has sent* the invitations. / Passive: The invitations *have been sent* by my cousin.
  - **Past Continuous**: Active: My uncle *was cleaning* the car. / Passive: The car *was being cleaned* by my uncle.
  - **Past Perfect Simple**: Active: Our neighbours *had taken* the twins to the zoo. / Passive: The twins *had been taken* to the zoo by our neighbours.
  - **Be Going To**: Active: They *’re going to invite* Phil to the party. / Passive: Phil *is going to be invited* to the party.
  - **Modals**: Active: They *might invite* Kyle to the party. / Passive: Kyle *might be invited* to the party.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Use *by* to emphasize who: Ex: My sister’s bedroom *was painted* *by* my parents.
  - Use *with* to emphasize what: Ex: Soup *is usually eaten* *with* a spoon.
  - Omit *by*/*with* if unnecessary/unknown: Ex: Mrs Fisher *was taken* to hospital yesterday.

# 7 Nouns and Articles

## 7.1 Countable Nouns
- **Definition**: Have singular/plural forms and take singular/plural verbs.
- **Examples**: *shop/shops*, *baby/babies*, *dish/dishes*
- **Helpful Hints**: Use with: *a*, *an*, *a few*, *many*, *one, two, etc*
- **Watch out**: Irregular plurals: *child/children*, *person/people*, *foot/feet*, *tooth/teeth*, *man/men*, *woman/women*

## 7.2 Uncountable Nouns
- **Definition**: Cannot be counted, no plural form, take singular verbs.
- **Examples**: *advice*, *bread*, *fruit*, *furniture*, *hair*, *homework*, *information*, *money*, *news*, *paper*, *rice*, *work*
- **Examples**: My money *is* in my wallet. / The news *was* a complete shock.
- **Watch out**:
  - Some uncountable nouns are plural: *clothes*, *jeans*. Ex: Your clean clothes *are* on the bed.
  - Some nouns have countable/uncountable meanings: Ex: *Get me some paper* (uncountable) vs. *Get me a paper* (newspaper).
- **Helpful Hints**: Use with: *a little*, *much*, *a bit of*, *a piece of*, *a lot of*, *some*, *lots of*, *the*, *any* (in questions/negatives).

## 7.3 Articles

#### A (Indefinite Article)
- **Use**: Singular countable nouns (not specific)
- **Example**: I *need* to *get* a new coat.

#### An (Indefinite Article)
- **Use**: Instead of *a* when the next word starts with a vowel sound
- **Example**: I *don’t have* enough money for an expensive dress.
- **Watch out**: Depends on sound, not spelling: *an honest person*, *an hour*, *a euro*, *a uniform*.

#### The (Definite Article)
- **Use**: Specific singular/plural countable nouns, specific uncountable nouns
- **Example**: Let *’s go* to the new shopping centre. / I *gave* the shop assistant the money.

#### No Article (Zero Article)
- **Use**: General plural countable nouns, general uncountable nouns
- **Example**: Prices *have gone up* a lot recently. / Fresh fruit *is* really good for you.

#### Special Rules
- **Places**:
  - *the*: *the Atlantic*, *the Amazon*, *the Antarctic*, *the USA*, *the UK*, *the theatre*, *the Earth*, *the world*, *the sky*, *the moon*, *the sun*, *the sea*, *the environment*
  - No article: *Moscow*, *France*, *Europe*, *Baker Street*, *Mars*
- **Activities**:
  - *a/an*: *have a job*, *work as a ...*
  - *the*: *on the radio*, *the media*, *play the piano*
  - No article: *go to work*, *on TV*, *go shopping*, *play tennis*, *listen to music*, *go to school*, *be at school/university*, *maths*
- **Time**:
  - *the*: *in the morning/afternoon/evening*, *on the 20th March*, *in the 1950s*
  - No article: *Thursday*, *May*, *2009*, *at night*
- **People**:
  - *the*: *the King*, *the Prime Minister*, *the army*, *the navy*, *the police*, *the Germans*, *the English*
  - No article: *become king*, *he’s English*, *speak English*

# 8 Modals

## 8.1 Introduction to Modals
- **Modal Verbs**: *can*, *may*, *will*, *shall*, *must*, *could*, *might*, *would*, *should*
- **Form**:
  - **Statement**: *can*, *may*, *will*, *shall*, *must*
  - **Negative**: *can’t/cannot*, *may not*, *won’t/will not*, *shan’t/shall not*, *mustn’t/must not*
  - **Statement/Negative**: *could/couldn’t*, *might/mightn’t*, *would/wouldn’t*, *should/shouldn’t*
- **Characteristics**:
  - One form: Ex: I/you/he/she/it/we/they *may write* an e-mail.
  - Followed by bare infinitive: Ex: You *should call* Stella.
  - No infinitive.
- **Semi-Modals**: *ought to*, *have to*, *need to*
  - *ought to* doesn’t change.
  - *have to*/*need to* change like normal verbs and have infinitives.
- **Watch out**:
  - Questions: Ex: *Can* you *understand* what he *’s saying*?
  - Passive: Ex: The address *should be written* clearly on the front of the envelope.

## 8.2 Modals 1: Ability, Permission, Advice
- **Ability**:
  - *can* (now/generally): Ex: *Can* you *use* a fax machine?
  - *could* (past): Ex: Tom *could read* when he was two years old.
  - *be able to* (other tenses): Ex: It *’s* useful to *be able to order* things by e-mail.
- **Permission**:
  - Asking: *can/could/may*. Ex: *Can/Could/May* I *use* the phone?
  - Giving: *can/may*. Ex: You *can/may send* the fax when you like.
- **Watch out**: *May* is more polite than *could*, which is more polite than *can*.

## 8.3 Modals 2: Obligation, Probability, Possibility
- **Obligation**:
  - Present/future: *must/mustn’t*, *have to*, *need to*. Ex: All visitors *must turn off* their mobile phones.
  - No obligation: *don’t have to*, *don’t need to*, *needn’t*. Ex: You *don’t have to* pay to send an e-mail.
  - Past: *had to*. Ex: Yesterday, Sam *had to buy* more stamps.
  - No past obligation: *didn’t have to*, *didn’t need to*. Ex: I *didn’t have to* use Italian.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - *have to* is more common in spoken English; *must* is used in written notices.
  - *mustn’t* (prohibited) vs. *don’t have to* (not necessary).
- **Probability and Possibility**:
  - Strong probability: *must*, *can’t*, *couldn’t*. Ex: The phone *is ringing* – it *must be* Simon.
  - Present/future probability: *should*, *ought to*. Ex: We *ought to* hear from Cheryl this weekend.
  - Possibility: *could*, *may*, *might*. Ex: It *could/may/might be* Polish.
- **Helpful Hints**: Use *must*, *can’t*, *couldn’t* for evidence-based probability.

## 8.4 Modals 3: The Modal Perfect
- **Form**: *modal + have + past participle*
- **Examples**:
  - **Statement**: You *should have told* me you were going shopping.
  - **Negative**: You *shouldn’t have told* Liz what Bill said.
  - **Question**: *Should* I *have invited* Carol to the party?
- **Use**:
  - Ability (missed opportunity): *could*. Ex: We *could have gone* to the party, but we didn’t.
  - Criticism: *ought to*, *should*. Ex: You *should have invited* Carol. (Wrong not to)
  - Probability: *must*, *can’t*. Ex: They *must have had* a lovely holiday!
  - Possibility: *could*, *may*, *might*. Ex: Helen *might have found* a new house.
  - Expectation: *ought to*, *should*. Ex: Jim *should have arrived* half an hour ago.
- **Watch out**:
  - *could have* (missed opportunity) vs. *could* (general past ability): Ex: I *could play* the guitar at seven. / I *could have played* the guitar.
  - Irregular past participles (see page 182).

# 9 Questions, Question Tags, Indirect Questions

## 9.1 Questions
- **Normal Main Verbs**:
  - Simple tenses: Ex: *Do* you *feel* cold? / *Did* they *go* shopping?
  - Continuous tenses: Ex: *Am* I *annoying* you? / *Were* they *waiting* for you?
  - Perfect tenses: Ex: *Have* you *seen* this film? / *Had* it *started*?
- **Be as a Main Verb**: Ex: *Am* I *late*? / *Have* you *been* ill?
- **Have as a Main Verb**: Ex: *Does* she *have* a bath every day?
- **Modals**: Ex: *Should* I *call* the police?
- **Question Words**: Ex: *Who was* in prison? / *Where do* they *live*?
- **Watch out**:
  - Passive questions: Auxiliary before subject. Ex: *Was* Mr Jenkins *arrested* yesterday?
  - *Who*/*What* as object uses *do*: Ex: *Who did* you *tell*?

## 9.2 Question Tags
- **Use**: To ask for agreement or check truth
- **Examples**:
  - Simple tenses: Ex: Phil *works* here, *doesn’t* he?
  - Continuous tenses: Ex: You *’re coming*, *aren’t* you?
  - Perfect tenses: Ex: They *’ve gone*, *haven’t* they?
  - *Be*: Ex: He *’s* new here, *isn’t* he?
  - *Have*: Ex: They *have* a car, *haven’t/don’t* they?
  - Modals: Ex: Jan *should be* here by now, *shouldn’t* she?
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - *I am* → *aren’t I*? / *I’m not* → *am I*?
  - With *Let’s*: Ex: Let *’s do* the washing-up later, *shall* we?

## 9.3 Indirect Questions
- **Use**: Polite questions
- **Form**: Phrase + clause with normal word order
- **Examples**:
  - *Can/Could* you *tell* me where the bank *is*?
  - *Do* you *know* if Alison *lives* there?
  - *I wonder* if you *could tell* me where the toilets *are*.

# 10 So and Such, Too and Enough

## 10.1 So and Such
- **Use**: Show results of a situation/action
- **Form**:
  - With adjective: *so + adjective + that*. Ex: Al *is so good that* he was made manager.
  - With noun: *so + many/much + noun + that*. Ex: Al *sells so many cars that* he was made manager.
  - *such + a/an + adjective + noun + that*. Ex: Al *is such a good worker that* he was made manager.
  - *such + adjective + plural noun + that*. Ex: Al *gets such good results that* he was made manager.
  - *such + a lot of + noun + that*. Ex: Al *makes such a lot of money* for the company *that* he was made manager.
  - With adverb: *so + adverb + that*. Ex: Al *works so well that* he was made manager.

## 10.2 Too and Enough
- **Use**: Describe excess (*too*) or sufficiency (*enough*)
- **Form**:
  - With adjective: *too + adjective (+ full infinitive)* / *adjective + enough (+ full infinitive)*. Ex: It *’s too cold to work* in the garden. / Carol *isn’t patient enough to work* as a teacher.
  - With noun: *too + many/much + noun (+ full infinitive)* / *enough + noun (+ full infinitive)*. Ex: Jonty *works too many hours to have* any hobbies. / We *don’t have enough money to pay* our bills.
  - With adverb: *too + adverb (+ full infinitive)* / *adverb + enough (+ full infinitive)*. Ex: I *got* there *too late to see* the manager. / Jack *did* the work *quickly enough to finish* early.
- **Watch out**:
  - *Too* ≠ *very*. Use *too* for negative effects: Ex: I *’ve got too much work*. (Not: *This job is too much fun.*)
  - *Enough* follows adjective: Ex: You *’re not old enough* to work here. (Not: *You’re not enough old.*)
  - Can use *for*: Ex: It *’s too hot for me* in this office.

# 11 Comparatives and Superlatives

## 11.1 Comparatives
- **Use**: Compare different things/people/actions
- **Form**:
  - One syllable: *+ -er*. Ex: *hard* → *harder*
  - One syllable ending in -e: *+ -r*. Ex: *late* → *later*
  - One syllable, vowel + consonant: *double consonant + -er*. Ex: *big* → *bigger*
  - Two syllables ending in -y: *-y → -ier*. Ex: *pretty* → *prettier*
  - Two or more syllables: *more/less + adjective*. Ex: *more/less interesting*
  - Irregular: *good* → *better*, *bad* → *worse*, *little* → *less*, *far* → *farther/further*, *many/much* → *more*
  - Adverbs:
    - Regular: *more/less + adverb*. Ex: *more/less carefully*
    - Irregular: *well* → *better*, *badly* → *worse*, *near* → *nearer*, *fast* → *faster*, *late* → *later*, *far* → *farther/further*
- **Example**: My new job *is more enjoyable than* my old one.
- **Helpful Hints**: Often followed by *than*.

## 11.2 Superlatives
- **Use**: Compare one member to the whole group
- **Form**:
  - One syllable: *+ -est*. Ex: *hard* → *hardest*
  - One syllable ending in -e: *+ -st*. Ex: *late* → *latest*
  - One syllable, vowel + consonant: *double consonant + -est*. Ex: *big* → *biggest*
  - Two syllables ending in -y: *-y → -iest*. Ex: *pretty* → *prettiest*
  - Two or more syllables: *most/least + adjective*. Ex: *most/least interesting*
  - Irregular: *good* → *best*, *bad* → *worst*, *little* → *least*, *far* → *farthest/furthest*, *many/much* → *most*
  - Adverbs:
    - Regular: *most/least + adverb*. Ex: *most/least carefully*
    - Irregular: *well* → *best*, *badly* → *worst*, *near* → *nearest*, *fast* → *fastest*, *late* → *latest*, *far* → *farthest/furthest*
- **Example**: John *’s* job *is* the *hardest*.

# 12 Conditionals

## 12.1 Introduction to Conditionals
- **Definition**: Sentences with *if* have two parts: situation (*if* clause) and result.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - *If* at start: Use comma. Ex: *If* you *join* a gym, I *’ll join* too.
  - Result first: No comma. Ex: I *’ll join* too *if* you *join* a gym.

## 12.2 Zero Conditional
- **Form**: *if + present simple, present simple*
- **Use**: General or scientific facts
- **Example**: *If* people *eat* too much, they often *get* fat.

## 12.3 First Conditional
- **Form**: *if + present simple, will + bare infinitive*
- **Use**: Real or likely situations and their results
- **Example**: *If* you *take* these pills, you *’ll start* to feel better very soon.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Can use other modals: Ex: *If* you *get* some rest, you *might feel* better tomorrow.
  - Can use imperative: Ex: *If* you *don’t feel* well, *go* home!

## 12.4 Second Conditional
- **Form**: *if + past simple, would + bare infinitive*
- **Use**: Impossible or unlikely situations and their results
- **Example**: *If* my legs *were* longer, I *would be* a much faster runner!
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Used for advice with *If I were/was you*: Ex: *If I were you*, I *would eat* less chocolate. (*were* more formal)

## 12.5 Third Conditional
- **Form**: *if + past perfect simple, would + have + past participle*
- **Use**: Unreal past situations and results
- **Example**: *If* the chemist *had been* open, I *would have bought* some aspirin.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Can use *could*/*might*: Ex: *If* you *had eaten* a giant pizza, you *might have been* sick!
- **Watch out**: Only conditional referring to the past. Ex: *If I had had* a headache, I *would have taken* an aspirin. (Past)

# 13 Reported Speech

## 13.1 Reported Speech
- **Use**: Report what someone else said
- **Form**:
  - Direct → Reported:
    - Present simple: *I want to build a new house,* → Jill *said* she *wanted* to build a new house.
    - Present continuous: *We are making a dress,* → They *said* they *were making* a dress.
    - Present perfect continuous: *I have been drawing all day,* → Debbie *said* she *had been drawing* all day.
    - Past simple: *Jim made a card for me yesterday,* → Amy *said* Jim *had made* a card for her the day before.
    - Past continuous: *I was writing a poem,* → Tina *said* she *had been writing* a poem.
    - *will*: *I will make you a scarf,* → My grandma *said* she *would make* me a scarf.
    - *am/is/are going to*: *They are going to make a new one,* → Joe *said* they *were going to make* a new one.
    - *can*: *I can draw quite well,* → Emma *said* she *could draw* quite well.
    - *must/have to*: *You have to visit the fashion show,* → She *said* we *had to visit* the fashion show.
    - *may*: *I may visit the White House next week,* → Polly *said* she *might visit* the White House the following week.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - Change pronouns, time, and place words:
    - *you* → *I/me/they/them*, *here* → *there*, *now* → *then*, *tomorrow* → *the next day*, etc.
  - No tense change if reporting verb is present: Ex: Tony *says* he *is going to study* architecture.
  - No change for *past perfect*, *would*, *should*, *could*, *might*.
  - Other verbs: *apologise*, *deny*, *promise*, *refuse*, *suggest*. Ex: Jan *apologised* for *losing* the picture.

## 13.2 Reported Questions, Orders, Requests
- **Use**: Report questions, orders, or requests
- **Examples**:
  - Questions with *have*/*do*/*be*: *Have you been to the gallery?* → He *asked* her *if* she *had been* to the gallery.
  - Modal questions: *Can you paint?* → Mary *asked* her friend *if* he *could paint*.
  - Question words: *What kind of shoes are in fashion now?* → My mum *asked* me *what kind of shoes were* in fashion at that moment.
  - Orders: *Put your clothes in the drawer,* → Mum *told* me *to put* my clothes in the drawer.
  - Requests: *Will you make me one?* → I *asked* Terry *to make* me one.
- **Helpful Hints**: Can use *whether* instead of *if*.
- **Watch out**: Use normal word order: Ex: I *asked when* they *would finish* the house. (Not: *when would they finish*)

# 14 Direct and Indirect Objects
- **Verbs Without an Object**:
  - Ex: *fall down*, *get up*, *happen*, *laugh*, *grow*, *run away*, *sit down*, *sleep*, *speak*, *stand up*, *walk*, *work*
  - Example: The flowers *grew*.
- **Verbs With One Object**:
  - Ex: *borrow*, *drink*, *have*, *paint*, *close*, *drive*, *invite*, *pick up*, *plant*, *draw*, *eat*, *open*
  - Example: We *picked up* the rubbish.
- **Verbs With Two Objects**:
  - Direct (thing) + indirect (person): Ex: Simon *gave* Jill some flowers.
  - Direct + preposition (*to*/*for*) + indirect: Ex: Simon *gave* some flowers *to* Jill.
  - Verbs: *bring*, *buy*, *get*, *give*, *lend*, *make*, *offer*, *owe*, *pass*, *pay*, *read*, *send*, *show*, *sing*, *take*, *teach*, *tell*, *throw*, *write*
  - Passive: Ex: Jill *was given* some flowers. / Some flowers *were given* to Jill.
- **Watch out**: No preposition if indirect object comes first: Ex: *Simon gave to Jill some flowers.* (Wrong)

# 15 Wish
- **Use**: Talk about unreal situations we want to be real
- **Form**:
  - Now/generally: *wish + past simple*. Ex: Carl *wishes* he *had* a telescope.
  - Past: *wish + past perfect simple*. Ex: Tracy *wishes* she *’d seen* that programme.
  - Criticism/complaints: *wish + would + bare infinitive*. Ex: I *wish* people *would throw* their litter in the bin!
  - Ability/permission: *wish + could + bare infinitive*. Ex: I *wish* I *could travel* through time!
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - *was*/*were* with *wish + past simple*: Ex: I *wish* I *was/were* an astronaut. (*were* more formal)
  - Use *if only* like *wish*: Ex: *If only* I *was/were* an astronaut.
- **Watch out**:
  - No *would* for self-wishes: Ex: *I wish I would live on Mars.* (Wrong)
  - Use *hope* for possible future: Ex: I *hope* it *doesn’t snow* tomorrow.

# 16 -ing and Infinitive

## 16.1 -ing
- **Use**: After certain verbs: *admit*, *avoid*, *deny*, *discuss*, *dislike*, *enjoy*, *feel like*, *finish*, *give up*, *mention*, *mind*, *practise*, *suggest*, *take up*
- **Example**: He *enjoys making* other people laugh.
- **Helpful Hints**:
  - After prepositions: Ex: I *’m afraid of flying*.
  - Phrases ending in *to*: Ex: I *look forward to hearing* from you.
  - As subject: Ex: *Cooking is* great fun!

## 16.2 Infinitive
- **Full Infinitive**: After verbs like *advise*, *afford*, *agree*, *choose*, *decide*, *expect*, *help*, *hope*, *invite*, *learn*, *manage*, *offer*, *plan*, *pretend*, *promise*, *refuse*, *seem*, *teach*, *tell*, *want*, *would like*
  - Ex: I *decided to apologise* to Emma.
- **Bare Infinitive**: After *let*, *make* (+ object). Ex: He *wants* me *to tell* him a joke.
- **Watch out**: Passive *make* uses full infinitive: Ex: Michael *was made to apologise* by his mother.

## 16.3 -ing or Infinitive
- **Same/Nearly Same Meaning**: *begin*, *continue*, *hate*, *like*, *love*, *prefer*, *start*. Ex: I *started liking/to like* James.
- **Different Meaning**:
  - *remember*: + *ing* (past memory): *Do* you *remember seeing* that comedy? / + infinitive (planned action): *Did* you *remember to say* sorry?
  - *forget*: + *ing* (can’t recall): I *’d forgotten hearing* that joke. / + infinitive (failed to do): I *forgot to invite* Shelly!
  - *stop*: + *ing* (cease): *Stop crying*. / + infinitive (interrupt): I *stopped to get* her some flowers.
  - *try*: + *ing* (experiment): *Have* you *tried talking* to her? / + infinitive (effort): I *’m trying to say* I’m sorry.

# 17 Both, Either, Neither, So, Nor

## 17.1 Both
- **Form**: *both + noun + and + noun* / *both + adjective + and + adjective*
- **Use**: Emphasize two things are true
- **Example**: *Both* Adam *and* Vicky *said* Colin was very kind. / Simon *is both rude and unkind*.
- **Helpful Hints**: Use *of* before pronouns: Ex: I *’ve had* an argument with *both of them*.

## 17.2 Either
- **Form**: *either + noun + or + noun* / *either + adjective + or + adjective* / *either + verb + or + verb*
- **Use**: Choice between two things
- **Example**: I *’ll buy* a Valentine’s card with *either* a puppy *or* a kitten on it.
- **Helpful Hints**: Use *of* before pronouns: Ex: I *’ll go out* with *either of them*.

## 17.3 Neither
- **Form**: *neither + noun + nor + noun* / *neither + adjective + nor + adjective* / *neither + verb + nor + verb*
- **Use**: Two negative things are true
- **Example**: I *’ve got neither* the time *nor* the energy to take up a new hobby.
- **Helpful Hints**: Use *of* before pronouns: Ex: *Neither of us found* Jason’s joke funny.

## 17.4 So, Nor
- **Form**: *so + do/have/be/modal + subject* / *nor + do/have/be/modal + subject*
- **Use**:
  - *so*: Add to positive statement: Ex: Lisa *is* really unkind, and *so is* Angela.
  - *nor*: Add to negative statement: Ex: My brother *hasn’t been invited* to the party, and *nor has* his friend.
- **Helpful Hints**: Can be used alone: Ex: *I like Hannah’s sense of humour.* *So do I.*

# 18 Connectives

## 18.1 Time Words and Phrases
- **Use**: Present simple for future with: *after*, *as soon as*, *before*, *until/till*, *when*, *while*
- **Examples**:
  - I *’ll call* you *after* we *solve* the problem.
  - It *’ll be* great *when* we *find* the solution.
- **Watch out**: Can start sentence: Ex: *As soon as* we *solve* the problem, I *’ll call* you.

## 18.2 Although
- **Use**: Express contrast
- **Form**: *Although + subject + verb, subject + verb*
- **Example**: *Although* my homework *was* difficult, I *finished* it before bed.
- **Watch out**: Can be in middle: Ex: I *finished* my homework before bed, *although* it *was* difficult.

## 18.3 In Spite of/Despite
- **Use**: Express contrast
- **Form**:
  - *In spite of/despite + -ing, subject + verb*. Ex: *In spite of revising* for hours, I *didn’t do* well in the test.
  - *In spite of/despite + noun, subject + verb*. Ex: *Despite* my revision, I *didn’t do* well in the test.
- **Watch out**: Can be in middle: Ex: I *didn’t do* well in the test, *despite revising* for hours.

## 18.4 However
- **Use**: Express contrast
- **Form**: *Subject + verb. However, subject + verb.*
- **Example**: We *believed* that we *would find* a solution. *However*, we *were* wrong.
- **Watch out**: Can be at end: Ex: We *were* wrong, *however*.

## 18.5 Unless
- **Use**: Means *if… not* or *except if*
- **Example**: *Unless* you *hurry up*, we *’ll be* late.
