# **How to use this handbook** 

* Study the book in order if your foundations are weak: sentence structure, noun phrases, articles, and verb forms come before advanced writing or speaking sections. 

* Do not only read rules. Say the model sentences aloud, then write new sentences with your own information, daily routines, opinions, or work/study topics. 

* When you meet a mistake that you often make, copy it into an error log. Write the wrong sentence, the corrected sentence, and one new example. 

* Use the writing sections to build paragraphs and short answers. Use the speaking sections to practise chunks, follow-up questions, and repair strategies. 

* For vocabulary, learn words in groups: word family, common collocations, prepositions, typical sentence pattern, and a personal example. 

* Return to the common-mistake tables often. Vietnamese learners usually improve fastest when they stop translating word by word and start noticing English patterns. 

 

## **Core principle** 

• Do not study grammar and vocabulary as separate subjects. Use grammar to build real sentences and use vocabulary in fixed phrases and natural collocations.

# **Why English feels difficult for Vietnamese learners** 

 English and Vietnamese organise meaning differently. If you understand the differences clearly, many repeated mistakes stop feeling random. The table below shows the contrasts that most often affect writing and speaking. 

| Feature  | Difference  | Practical result  |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Subjects  | Vietnamese can drop the subject when the context is clear; English usually needs an explicit subject.  | Say “It is raining,” not just “Is raining.”  |
| Verb tense  | Vietnamese often shows time with adverbs like đã, đang, sẽ; English usually marks time on the verb too.  | Do not rely only on yesterday/now/tomorrow. Change the verb form as well.  |
| Articles  | Vietnamese has no article system like a, an, the.  | Vietnamese learners often forget articles or use the too often.  |
| Plural forms  | Vietnamese nouns do not change like book/books in the same way English nouns do.  | Listen for final \-s and write plural forms carefully.  |
| Be  | Vietnamese often says a sentence without an equivalent of am/is/are.  | Do not write “She very friendly.” Write “She is very friendly.”  |
| Question form  | Vietnamese makes questions with particles and voice change; English changes word order or uses do/does/did.  | Practise “Do you…?”, “Are you…?”, “Have you…?”  |
| Adjective order  | Vietnamese adjectives often come after the noun; English adjectives usually come before the noun.  | Say “a useful book,” not “a book useful.”  |
| Final sounds  | Vietnamese speakers often drop final consonants in fast speech.  | Missing 	final 	sounds 	can 	hide 	grammar:  works/worked/books/asked.  |
| Direct translation  | Some natural Vietnamese expressions become unnatural in English if translated word for word.  | Use chunks such as “turn on the light,” not “open the light.”  |

 

## **High-priority habits** 

* Always write the subject and verb first. 

* Check articles and plural forms before you check difficult vocabulary. 

* Learn words as chunks, not as single translations. 

* Read your English aloud; speaking reveals grammar gaps very quickly.

# **Thinking in English, not translated Vietnamese** 

Build sentence foundations, word order, noun control, and the patterns that Vietnamese learners need first. 

 

## **1\. The shortest complete English sentence** 

 **Practical goal:** *Build sentences that are complete, grammatical, and easy to understand in both speech and writing.* 

 Every useful piece of English starts with a complete thought. In normal English, a complete sentence needs a subject and a verb. Sometimes the verb is an action verb, and sometimes it is a form of be. Vietnamese often allows shorter answers and topic-based fragments when the context is clear, but English requires a clearer sentence frame more often. This is why many Vietnamese learners produce lines such as “Very difficult” or “Because too expensive” when they should produce a full sentence. 

 A good first habit is to check three things: Who or what is the sentence about? What is happening or being described? Is the idea finished? If the answer to any of these questions is no, the sentence probably needs more work. Complete sentences matter in conversation because they make your meaning clear; they matter in writing because incomplete sentences reduce accuracy and coherence. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* English usually needs a visible subject: I, you, we, they, he, she, it, this, the problem, my friend, the chart, the company. 

* The verb can be an action verb (work, study, change) or a linking verb (be, seem, become, look, feel). 

* A phrase is not always a sentence. “In my hometown” is only a phrase. “I grew up in my hometown” is a sentence. 

* Fragments often appear after because, although, when, if, and while. Add the main clause. 

 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Subject \+ verb  | basic complete idea  | Prices increased.  |
| Subject \+ be \+ adjective  | describe a state or quality  | The room is quiet.  |
| Subject \+ be \+ noun  | identify or classify  | My brother is an engineer.  |
| Subject \+ verb \+ object  | show an action affecting something  | She finished her report.  |
| There is/are \+ noun  | introduce something  | There are many small cafés nearby.  |
| It is \+ adjective \+ to-infinitive  | evaluate an action  | It is useful to review daily.  |

 

 	 

### **Useful speaking examples** 

* I work in sales, but I am studying data analysis at night. 

* My English is not perfect yet, but it is improving. 

* There are too many motorbikes on this street in the morning. 

* It is hard to stay confident when I forget a word. 

### **Useful writing examples** 

* The main reason is that public transport is still limited in many areas. 

* There are several factors that explain this change. 

* It is important to separate opinion from evidence in formal writing. 

* The chart shows a gradual increase over the ten-year period. 

### **Common mistakes and better English** 

| Common mistake  | Better English  | Why  |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Very convenient for students.  | It is very convenient for students.  | English needs a subject and a verb.  |
| Because the price too high.  | Because the price is too high, many people delay the purchase.  | A because-clause is not a full sentence by itself.  |
| In my city many coffee shops.  | There are many coffee shops in my city.  | Use 	there 	is/are 	to 	introduce existence.  |
| My father engineer.  | My father is an engineer.  | Use be to connect subject and noun.  |

## **2\. Word order: English is less flexible than Vietnamese** 

 	**Practical goal:** *Put words in a natural order so your sentence sounds English, not translated.* 

 English depends heavily on word order. The subject usually comes before the verb. Adjectives usually come before the noun. Adverbs of frequency usually come before the main verb but after be. Time and place often come later in the sentence, although some adverbials can move for emphasis. Vietnamese can be more flexible in information order because meaning is supported by context. English can become unclear quickly if words appear in the wrong place. 

 For Vietnamese learners, word order problems often appear in three areas: adjective position, adverb position, and clause position. The fastest way to improve is to memorise sentence frames, not isolated rules. Think in blocks: subject \+ verb \+ object \+ place \+ time, or subject \+ be \+ adjective, or there is/are \+ noun \+ place. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert** 

* Put adjectives before nouns: a difficult exam, an interesting book, a useful website. 

* Put adverbs of frequency before the main verb but after be: I usually wake up early; She is often late. 

* Put enough after adjectives but before nouns: good enough, enough time. 

* Long noun phrases should stay close to the noun they describe. 

 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Adjective \+ noun  | describe the noun naturally  | a practical guide  |
| be \+ adverb of frequency  | frequency with be  | He is usually calm.  |
| adverb of frequency \+ main verb  | frequency with other verbs  | They often practise together.  |
| verb \+ object \+ place \+ time  | common neutral order  | We held the meeting online yesterday.  |
| linker \+ clause, main clause  | show logical relation  | Although it was late, she kept working.  |
| main clause \+ because/when/if- clause  | keep main idea first  | I stayed home because it was raining.  |

 

### **Useful speaking examples** 

* I usually review new words on the bus. 

* My office is quite far from my home, so I leave early. 

* We had a really useful discussion after class yesterday. 

* She is often nervous before presentations. 

### **Useful writing examples** 

* The survey reveals a clear difference between urban and rural respondents. 

* Students frequently struggle with article use in the early stages of learning. 

* Although the change was gradual, the overall trend was upward. 

* This approach provides a more practical solution for working adults. 

### **Common mistakes and better English** 

| Common mistake  | Better English  | Why  |
| :---- | ----- | ----- |
| a car red  | a red car  | Adjectives usually come before nouns in English.  |
| I go often there  | I often go there  | Adverbs of frequency usually go before the main verb.  |
| She speaks English very well in meetings usually  | She usually speaks English very well in meetings  | Put 	the 	frequency 	adverb earlier.  |
| I bought yesterday a new laptop  | I 	bought 	a 	new 	laptop  yesterday  | Object usually comes before the time expression.  |

## **3\. Nouns, articles, plurals, and quantity** 

 **Practical goal:** *Use noun forms correctly and stop making article and plural mistakes that are extremely common among Vietnamese learners.* 

 Nouns look simple, but they cause a high number of errors in both writing and speaking. English nouns can be countable or uncountable, singular or plural, specific or non-specific. That means you often need to decide three things at the same time: Do I need a/an/the? Do I need a plural form? Do I need a quantity word such as much, many, a little, a few, some, or several? 

 Vietnamese does not force the same decisions. Because of that, many learners write sentences such as “I need informations,” “teacher should give student feedback,” or “the life is difficult” when no article is needed. If you master noun patterns, your English immediately looks more mature and accurate. 

 	 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* Use a/an for one non-specific countable noun: a phone, an hour, a university, an MBA. 

* Use the when the listener/reader can identify the thing: the teacher we met, the chart, the internet, the city centre. 

* Most singular countable nouns need an article or determiner: a book, my book, this book, one book—not just book. 

* Uncountable nouns do not usually take plural \-s: information, advice, furniture, equipment, homework, research. 

 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| a/an \+ singular countable noun  | one item, non-specific  | a good idea  |
| the \+ noun  | specific item already known  | the main issue  |
| plural 	noun 	without  article  | general meaning  | Students need feedback.  |
| uncountable 	noun  without plural \-s  | mass or abstract noun  | Research takes time.  |
| many/few \+ countable  plural  | quantity with countable nouns  | many options  |
| much/little 	\+  uncountable noun  | quantity 	with 	uncountable nouns  | little time  |

 

### **Useful speaking examples** 

* I need some advice about choosing a course. 

* There are several problems with the current system. 

* The information on that website is not very clear. 

* I bought a new backpack because the old one was broken. 

 

### **Useful writing examples** 

* Students require access to reliable information and practical support. 

* The report identifies three major causes of the decline. 

* Many young people face financial pressure at university. 

* This equipment is expensive, but it improves efficiency. 

 	 

### **Common mistakes and better English** 

| Common mistake  | Better English  | Why  |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| I need many advices.  | I need a lot of advice.  | Advice is uncountable.  |
| Teacher should help student.  | Teachers should help students.  | General plural nouns are better here.  |
| She gave me an information.  | She gave me some information.  | Information is uncountable.  |
| I went to the school by bus yesterday to visit my cousin who studies there.  | I went to school by bus yesterday to visit my cousin who studies there.  | Go to school often has a general institutional meaning without the.  |
| Life in the Vietnam is changing quickly.  | Life in Vietnam is changing quickly.  | Country names usually do not take the.  |
| I bought book.  | I bought a book.  | Singular countable nouns usually need an article or determiner.  |

## **4\. Pronouns, possession, and reference** 

 	**Practical goal:** *Refer to people and things clearly, avoid repetition, and use possessive forms naturally.* 

 Pronouns help English sound smooth, but they also create confusion when the reference is unclear. A strong English sentence makes it easy for the listener or reader to know who he, she, they, it, this, or these refers to. Vietnamese can leave many references implicit, but English prefers clearer tracking, especially in writing. 

 You also need to master possessive forms because English uses them frequently: my, your, his, her, its, our, their, mine, yours, hers, ours, theirs, and apostrophe-s. Vietnamese learners often mix subject pronouns and possessive adjectives, or overuse of instead of shorter possessive structures. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* Do not confuse subject forms and possessive forms: she/her/hers; they/their/theirs. 

* Use it for things, ideas, weather, time, distance, and many impersonal sentences: It is late. It seems useful. 

* Use this/these for something psychologically or physically close; that/those for something more distant. 

* In writing, repeat the noun if the pronoun could be ambiguous. 

 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| subject pronoun \+ verb  | name the doer  | They agreed quickly.  |
| possessive adjective \+ noun  | show ownership  | our plan  |
| noun \+ 's \+ noun  | compact ownership  | Lan's presentation  |
| of \+ noun  | formal or inanimate possession  | the result of the survey  |
| this/these \+ noun  | point to something near/current  | these challenges  |
| one/ones  | avoid repetition of a noun  | the cheaper one  |

 

### **Useful speaking examples** 

* My manager changed her schedule, so our meeting starts later now. 

* I like these shoes, but that pair is more comfortable. 

* Their office is smaller than ours. 

* Lan's idea was simple, but it solved the problem. 

 

### **Useful writing examples** 

* This trend suggests that young adults are changing their priorities. 

* The company's strategy and its long-term goals are closely connected. 

* These findings support the argument presented in the previous section. 

* The quality of the data affects the reliability of the conclusion. 

### **Common mistakes and better English** 

| Common mistake  | Better English  | Why  |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| She forgot she laptop.  | She forgot her laptop.  | Use the possessive adjective her.  |
| The company changed their strategy and it lost customers.  | The company changed its strategy and then lost customers.  | Company as a singular organisation often takes its in formal writing.  |
| This problems are serious.  | These problems are serious.  | Plural noun needs these.  |
| The reason is because they lost their motivation, and this made students disappointed.  | The reason is that they lost their motivation, and this made the students disappointed.  | Use a clearer reference and avoid reason is because in formal writing.  |

## **5\. Adjectives, adverbs, and comparison** 

 	**Practical goal:** *Describe things accurately and stop confusing adjective and adverb forms.* 

 Adjectives describe nouns; adverbs usually describe verbs, adjectives, or whole clauses. This distinction is basic, but it creates frequent mistakes for Vietnamese learners because Vietnamese often uses one flexible word form in places where English changes the form. Another common problem is comparison: English uses comparative structures very often in both academic and daily English, so you need them for describing changes, making recommendations, and giving opinions. 

 This chapter also matters because good speaking and writing both depend on precise description. Strong descriptions make your answers sound more natural and your essays more analytical. Instead of saying good again and again, you need patterns such as more reliable, less effective, highly practical, relatively stable, and by far the most useful. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* Use adjectives after be and before nouns: useful advice, the result is useful. 

* Use adverbs for most verbs: speak clearly, write carefully, work efficiently. 

* Some words are both adjective and adverb or have unusual forms: fast, hard, late, early, well. 

* Do not confuse hard and hardly, late and lately, near and nearly. 

 

 	 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| adjective \+ noun  | describe a thing  | a clear explanation  |
| be \+ adjective  | describe a state  | The lesson is useful.  |
| verb \+ adverb  | describe manner  | She explained patiently.  |
| comparative \+ than  | compare two things  | This method is faster than the old one.  |
| the \+ superlative  | show the highest degree  | the most practical option  |
| as \+ adjective \+ as  | show equality  | It is as important as grammar.  |

 

### **Useful speaking examples** 

* This route is much faster than the other one in the morning. 

* He speaks so softly that I sometimes miss the last word. 

* Online lessons are convenient, but face-to-face classes can be more effective. 

* That was the most useful piece of feedback I received all week. 

### **Useful writing examples**

* Overall, the northern region remained relatively stable throughout the period. 

* This solution is not only practical but also environmentally friendly. 

* Students who revise regularly usually perform better than those who study only before exams.

| Common mistake  | Better English  | Why  |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| She speaks English very good.  | She speaks English very well.  | Use the adverb well after a verb.  |
| This is more easier.  | This is easier.  | Do not use double comparatives.  |
| My hometown is more peaceful than Ho Chi Minh City, especially at night.  | My hometown is more peaceful than Ho Chi Minh City, especially at night.  | Correct; keep as model of clean comparison.  |

## **6\. Prepositions in practical English** 

 **Practical goal:** *Use the most frequent prepositions correctly in time, place, movement, and fixed expressions.* 

 Prepositions cause frustration because they are partly logical and partly fixed. Some prepositions show a clear relationship in time or space: on Monday, at 7 p.m., in the kitchen, from home to work. Others belong to common word partnerships: interested in, depend on, good at, responsible for, arrive at, arrive in. Vietnamese learners often try to translate directly from Vietnamese, but English preposition choice usually depends on pattern memory. 

 The practical solution is not to memorise long theoretical lists. Instead, learn prepositions by category and by chunk. Learn not only arrive but arrive at the station, arrive in Hanoi, arrive home. Learn not only interested but interested in, interested in science, interested in learning how people think. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* Time: at \+ exact time, on \+ day/date, in \+ month/year/long period. 

* Place: at \+ point, in \+ enclosed area/city/country, on \+ surface or line. 

* Movement: to, into, onto, from, through, across, along, past. 

* Many common errors come from missing or extra prepositions: discuss something, enter a room, request something, marry someone. 

 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| at \+ time/place point  | exact point  | at 6.30; at the station  |
| on \+ day/surface  | days and surfaces  | on Friday; on the table  |
| in \+ period/area  | months, years, cities, countries  | in 2025; in Da Nang  |
| interested 	in/good  at/afraid of  | adjective \+ preposition  | good at maths  |
| depend 	on/belong  to/focus on  | verb \+ preposition  | focus on the key issue  |
| from ... to ...  | range or movement  | from home to work  |

* She is really good at explaining difficult ideas in simple language. 

* I usually work on my laptop in a small café near my apartment. 

* We walked across the bridge and into the old part of the city.

* Many graduates depend on part-time work while they are at university. 

* Interest in online learning increased sharply during the pandemic years.

Master helper verbs, tense choice, questions, verb patterns, passives, conditionals, and other structures that affect both writing and speaking. 

 

## **7\. Be, do, have, and other helper verbs** 

 **Practical goal:** *Control the helper verbs that make English questions, negatives, and many sentence patterns possible.* 

 A large part of English grammar depends on helper verbs, also called auxiliary verbs. Vietnamese learners often know the main verbs but still make errors because the helper system is different from Vietnamese. English uses be for identity, description, location, and continuous forms; do for questions, negatives, emphasis, and many common expressions; have for possession, obligations in some patterns, and perfect tenses. 

 If you do not control helper verbs, even simple English becomes unstable. This is why learners produce sentences such as “Why you late?”, “He not like coffee,” or “I very agree.” Strong control of be, do, and have creates a base for speaking naturally and for writing accurate sentences. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* Use be as the main verb in sentences like She is happy, They are at home, It was difficult. 

* Use do/does/did to make questions and negatives when there is no other auxiliary: Do you work here? She does not drive. 

* Use have as a main verb for possession and many common expressions: have a car, have breakfast, have trouble sleeping. 

* Agree is a normal verb, not an adjective. Say I agree, not I am agree. 

 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| be \+ noun/adjective/place  | identity, description, location  | He is a designer.  |
| do/does/did \+ subject \+ base verb  | questions  | Do you need help?  |
| subject \+ do/does/did not \+ base verb  | negatives  | They do not know.  |

## **8\. Present simple and present continuous** 

 	**Practical goal:** *Choose correctly between routine/fact meaning and current/temporary meaning.* 

 These two present forms are among the first tenses that learners study, but they remain a source of error for years. The present simple usually expresses habits, routines, repeated actions, general truths, and states. The present continuous usually expresses something happening now, around now, or in a temporary current period. In Vietnamese, the difference is often shown by time words or context, not by verb form alone, so English requires more conscious control. 

 A practical way to choose is to ask: Is this generally true, repeated, or stable? Use present simple. Is it happening now, developing at the moment, or temporary? Use present continuous. Some stative verbs, such as know, like, need, believe, belong, and understand, usually do not take the continuous form in normal use.

* Stative verbs are usually simple, not continuous: I know, I like, I need.

## **9\. Past simple, past continuous, and used to**

## **10\. Present perfect and past perfect** 

 	**Practical goal:** *Connect past actions to the present and show the order of past events accurately.* 

 The present perfect is one of the hardest tenses for Vietnamese learners because it mixes past time and present relevance. It often expresses experience, a recent result, change over time, or an unfinished period that continues to now. The past perfect is easier conceptually: it shows that one past event happened before another past event. Both tenses rely on have/has/had \+ past participle. 

 A useful practical rule is this: if you mention a finished past time such as yesterday, last year, or in 2018, you usually need the past simple. If the exact time is not important and the result matters now, the present perfect is often possible. For the past perfect, use it when the sequence would otherwise be unclear. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* Present perfect is common with ever, never, already, yet, just, recently, so far, over the last few years. 

* Do not combine the present perfect with finished past time expressions like yesterday or last week. 

* Past perfect often appears with before, after, by the time, when, and because one action happened earlier. 

* Learn irregular past participles carefully: gone, seen, done, made, written, spoken, become.

## **11\. Future forms: will, be going to, present continuous, and future time clauses** 

 **Practical goal:** *Talk about plans, predictions, decisions, schedules, and future conditions with the right form.* 

 English has several future forms because future meaning is not only about time; it is also about intention, evidence, schedule, and degree of certainty. Will often expresses a decision made at the moment of speaking, a promise, an offer, or a prediction. Be going to often expresses prior intention or a prediction based on present evidence. The present continuous often expresses arranged future plans. The present simple can also express schedules and timetables. In time clauses after when, before, after, until, and as soon as, English often uses a present form to refer to the future.

* After when/before/after/until/as soon as, use the present tense for future meaning: I'll call you when I arrive.

## **12\. Modal verbs: ability, advice, possibility, obligation, and politeness** 

 	**Practical goal:** *Express attitude and meaning more precisely than with basic present and past tenses alone.* 

 Modal verbs are powerful because they add meaning without changing the main action. They express ability, permission, obligation, advice, probability, deduction, willingness, and politeness. In everyday English, modals help you sound softer, clearer, and more natural. In writing, they help you avoid overstatement and express degrees of certainty. Vietnamese learners often know can and must, but practical English also depends on should, could, may, might, have to, need to, must not, don't have to, would, and be able to. 

 Many communication problems come from choosing a modal that is too strong or too weak. For example, must can sound very forceful, while should often sounds more suitable for advice. Could can be more polite than can in requests.

* Must not means prohibition; don't have to means lack of necessity. These meanings are very different. 

* Would is useful for polite requests, hypothetical situations, and repeated past willingness or preference.

## **14\. Gerunds and infinitives**

## **15\. Passive voice and impersonal structures** 

 	**Practical goal:** *Focus on results, processes, and actions when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or obvious.* 

 The passive voice is common in formal writing, reports, processes, and academic language because it shifts focus from the actor to the action or result. It is made with be \+ past participle. Many Vietnamese learners either avoid the passive completely or overuse it. The key is to use it when the receiver of the action is more important than the doer, or when the doer is unknown or unnecessary. 

 English also uses impersonal structures such as it is believed that, it is often argued that, people say that, and there is/are. These structures are practical for writing because they help you sound neutral and analytical. In speaking, passives can be useful too: I was told to wait, My order was cancelled, The road is being repaired. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* Passive \= be \+ past participle, and the be form changes with tense: is made, was built, has been improved, will be discussed. 

* Use by \+ agent only when the doer is important: The novel was written by Toni Morrison.

## **16\. Conditionals, relative clauses, and longer sentences** 

 **Practical goal:** *Express real and unreal conditions, add detail efficiently, and combine ideas into mature sentences.* 

 Longer English sentences are not simply a matter of adding more words. They require clear logical relationships between clauses. Conditionals help you express real possibilities, imagined situations, consequences, warnings, and recommendations. Relative clauses help you add information about a noun without starting a completely new sentence. These structures are essential for formal writing and for sophisticated speaking. 

 Vietnamese learners sometimes avoid these structures because shorter sentences feel safer. However, high-quality English depends on controlled complexity, not only simple accuracy. The goal is not to create very long sentences, but to create clean sentences in which every part has a purpose and the reader can still follow easily. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* First conditional: If \+ present simple, will \+ base verb. Real future possibility. 

* Second conditional: If \+ past simple, would \+ base verb. Imagined or less real situation now/future. 

* Third conditional: If \+ had \+ past participle, would have \+ past participle. Imagined past result. 

* Relative clauses use who, whom, whose, which, that, where, when. In defining clauses, that is common and commas are not used. 

 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| If \+ present, will \+ base  | real future conditional  | If you revise daily, you will improve.  |
| If \+ past, would \+ base  | hypothetical present/future  | If I had more time, I would travel more.  |
| If \+ had \+ pp, would have \+ pp  | hypothetical past  | If I had known, I would have called.  |
| **Pattern**  | **Use**  | **Example**  |
| noun \+ who/which/that \+ clause  | relative clause  | The book that you recommended was excellent.  |
| non-defining relative clause  | extra information with commas  | My brother, who lives in Hue, is visiting us.  |
| because/although/while/whereas/so that  | logical clause links  | Although it was expensive, we bought it.  |

 

### **Useful speaking examples** 

* If I had more free time, I would join a speaking club. 

* People who work long hours often find it hard to maintain healthy habits. 

* If the traffic is too heavy, I'll take the metro instead. 

* Although I was nervous at first, the presentation went better than I expected.

| ----- | ----- | ----- |

## **17\. Reported speech and reporting verbs** 

 	**Practical goal:** *Report what people said in a natural way for conversation, summaries, and formal writing.* 

 Reported speech allows you to tell someone what another person said without quoting their exact words. This skill is common in daily conversation, meetings, news summaries, and academic-style writing. In informal speech, you can often use simple patterns such as He said he was tired or She told me to wait. In more formal writing, reporting verbs such as argue, claim, suggest, explain, admit, recommend, and emphasise help you summarise ideas accurately. 

 Vietnamese learners sometimes avoid reported speech because of tense changes, pronoun changes, and time-word changes. The practical goal is not to memorise every possible shift at once; it is to handle the most useful patterns confidently. 

### **Vietnamese learner alert/Lưu ý cho người Việt** 

* Common patterns: say that..., tell someone that..., tell someone to..., ask whether/if..., ask someone to... 

* Backshifting often happens when the reporting verb is in the past: 'I am tired' → He said he was tired. 

* Time and place words may change: today → that day, tomorrow → the next day, here → there, now → then. 

* In writing, reporting verbs help you express stance: argue, suggest, note, point out, emphasise. 

 

### **Core patterns** 

| Pattern  | Use  | Example  |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| say that \+ clause  | report information  | She said that she was busy.  |
| tell \+ person \+ clause  | tell someone information  | He told me that the class was cancelled.  |
| tell/ask \+ person \+ to-infinitive  | commands and requests  | The manager asked us to wait.  |
| ask if/whether \+ clause  | yes/no reported question  | She asked whether I was free.  |
| ask \+ question word \+ clause  | reported information question  | He asked where I lived.  |
| reporting verb \+ that-clause  | formal source summary  | Researchers argue that sleep affects memory.  |

| ----- | ----- | ----- |

## **18\. Writing clear sentences: subject, verb, control** 

 Good writing does not start with long vocabulary lists; it starts with sentence control. A clear sentence has a stable structure, one main idea, and a reader-friendly order. Many Vietnamese learners know enough vocabulary to express an idea, but their writing still feels weak because the sentence frame is unstable. The most common problems are missing subjects, weak verb choices, too many ideas in one sentence, and direct translation from Vietnamese order. 

 When you write, first choose the core message. Then build it with a strong verb. After that, add detail in the order that makes the sentence easy to read. If a sentence becomes too long, cut it into two sentences or turn one part into a phrase. 

### **Key principles** 

* Make the main idea visible early. Do not hide the subject and verb inside a long phrase. 

* Prefer a clear verb to a weak structure with many nouns. Compare: The city experienced rapid growth, not The city had a rapid growth. 

* Use one sentence for one main step of thought. If you add too many because/which/and parts, the sentence becomes hard to control. 

* Read the sentence aloud. If you need extra breaths, the sentence may need to be divided or simplified. 

* After you draft a paragraph, highlight every be verb. Some are necessary, but too many may mean your writing needs stronger verbs. 

### **Useful frames** 

| Function  | Frame  |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Main statement**  | The main reason is that ...  |
| **Cause and effect**  | ... because .../This leads to .../As a result, ...  |
| **Contrast**  | However, .../In contrast, .../Although ..., ...  |
| **Example**  | For example, .../A clear example of this is ...  |
| **Result \+ explanation**  | This change can be explained by ...  |
| **Evaluation**  | This approach is effective because ...  |

 

 	 

### **Common writing problems** 

| Problem  | Better choice  |
| ----- | ----- |
| **There are many people choose online courses because convenient.**  | Many people choose online courses because they are convenient.  |
| **The reason is because students are busy.**  | The reason is that students are busy.  |
| **This trend has a bad influence for young people.**  | This trend has a bad influence on young people.  |
| **Nowadays people they can study anywhere.**  | Nowadays people can study anywhere.  |

## **19\. Building paragraphs that actually develop an idea** 

 A paragraph is not just a group of sentences on the same topic. A strong paragraph has a focus, a plan, and a clear movement from point to support. In practical English writing, a simple paragraph often has four parts: topic sentence, explanation, example, and mini-conclusion or link forward. Vietnamese learners often write paragraphs that circle around one topic without a clear line of development. This makes the writing feel repetitive or unfocused. 

 If you can build strong paragraphs, essays, reports, emails, and exam answers all become easier. Instead of thinking about a full page, think about one useful paragraph at a time. **Key principles** 

* Start with a clear topic sentence. The reader should know the paragraph's main point immediately. 

* Use the middle of the paragraph to explain the point, not to change the topic. 

* Include evidence, an example, or a specific detail. General statements alone feel weak. 

* End with a sentence that shows the result, significance, or transition to the next idea. 

* Keep unity: if a sentence does not support the topic sentence, move it or delete it. 

### **Useful frames**

| Function  | Frame  |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Topic sentence**  | One major reason for this trend is that ...  |
| **Explanation**  | This is mainly because ...  |
| **Example**  | For instance, ...  |
| **Result**  | As a result, ...  |
| **Mini-conclusion**  | Therefore, it is clear that ...  |
| **Transition**  | This point also connects to ...  |

 

### **Common writing problems** 

| Problem  | Better choice  |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Paragraph starts with example only**  | Start with the main point first, then give the example.  |
| **Different ideas mixed together**  | Put one main controlling idea in each paragraph.  |
| **Repeating the same point with different words**  | Add explanation, evidence, or consequence instead of repetition.  |
| **Very short paragraph with one sentence only**  | Develop the point with at least one explanation and one example when possible.  |

## **20\. Linking ideas naturally, not mechanically** 

 Linking words are useful, but many learners overuse them or use them in the wrong place. Good writing is coherent because the logic is clear, not because every sentence begins with moreover or besides. Some relationships can be shown through grammar, pronouns, repetition of key words, or cause-and-effect verbs. Linkers should support the flow, not carry the whole paragraph alone. 

 For Vietnamese learners, the practical challenge is balance. Use a range of connectors, but choose them by function: addition, contrast, cause, result, example, concession, or summary. Learn the punctuation patterns too, because punctuation controls how linkers work in writing. **Key principles** 

* Choose the linker that matches the logic exactly: however for contrast, therefore for result, for example for illustration, although for concession. 

* Do not use too many sentence-initial linkers. Sometimes a simple pronoun or a repeated key noun is enough. 

* Remember punctuation: However, .../..., whereas .../Although ..., .../... because ... 

* Use clause linkers and sentence adverbs, not only one type. Mix because, although, while, which means, therefore, as a result, in contrast. 

### **Useful frames** 

| Function  | Frame  |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Addition**  | also, in addition, moreover, another point is that ...  |
| **Contrast**  | however, in contrast, whereas, although ...  |
| **Cause**  | because, since, due to, as a result of ...  |
| **Result**  | therefore, so, consequently, as a result ...  |
| **Example**  | for example, for instance, such as ...  |
| **Summary**  | overall, in short, in summary ...  |

 

### **Common writing problems** 

| Problem  | Better choice  |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Although ... but ...**  | Use although or but, not both together in the same clause pattern.  |
| **Because ... so ...**  | Use because or so, depending on the structure.  |
| **On the other hand used without a first hand**  | Use on the other hand only when you are clearly balancing another point.  |
| **Besides overused for formal addition**  | In formal writing, in addition or moreover is often safer.  |

## **21\. Formal and informal English: choosing the right register** 

 English changes according to situation. The language you use in a message to a friend is not the same as the language you use in a report, scholarship application, or email to a lecturer. Vietnamese learners often produce writing that is grammatically correct but too conversational, or speaking that is too formal and distant. Register control means matching your language to the relationship, purpose, and setting. 

 Formal English is usually more explicit, more precise, and less emotional. Informal English is shorter, more direct, and more flexible. Good learners can move between the two styles when needed. **Key principles** 

* In formal writing, avoid slang, very personal fillers, and vague words like stuff, things, nice, bad when a more precise word exists. 

* In informal messages, shorter sentences and contractions are normal: I'm, don't, can't, thanks, see you soon. 

* Formal requests often use would, could, I would appreciate, I am writing to, I would be grateful if ... 

* Choose verbs carefully: buy/purchase, get/receive, help/assist, start/commence. Do not force formal words where simple words are clearer.

## **23\. Editing for grammar, vocabulary, and punctuation** 

 Strong writers do not only write; they revise. Editing is where grammar knowledge becomes practical skill. Many Vietnamese learners read their own writing only for meaning: they know what they wanted to say, so they miss small errors. Good editing means reading with a checklist and looking at one layer at a time. First check sentence structure. Then check verb forms. Then noun forms and articles. Then prepositions, pronouns, word choice, spelling, and punctuation. 

 Do not try to fix everything at once. Use passes. Each pass has a single question. This method is faster and more reliable than staring at the same paragraph and hoping to notice every mistake. **Key principles** 

* Pass 1: subject \+ verb \+ complete sentence. 

* Pass 2: tense, agreement, helper verbs, and verb patterns. 

* Pass 3: articles, plural forms, countability, and pronouns. 

* Pass 4: collocations, prepositions, register, and word choice. 

* Pass 5: punctuation, capitals, spacing, and spelling. 

### **Useful frames** 

| Function  | Frame  |
| ----- | ----- |
| **Check question**  | Does every sentence have a clear subject and a correct verb?  |
| **Verb check**  | Is the tense consistent with the time meaning?  |
| **Noun check**  | Do singular countable nouns have articles or determiners?  |
| **Word choice check**  | Is this the natural collocation?  |
| **Punctuation check**  | Do I need a comma after this linker?  |
| **Final read**  | Would I say this sentence to a real person?  |

 

### **Common writing problems** 

| Problem  | Better choice  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Many small errors left after first draft**  | Use a systematic editing order.  |
| **Correct grammar but unnatural collocation**  | Check word partnerships, not only grammar.  |
| **One long sentence with several hidden errors**  | Split the sentence first, then edit each part.  |
| **Problem**  | **Better choice**  |
| **No final read aloud**  | Reading aloud reveals rhythm, missing words, and awkward repetition.  |

## **24\. Common written error patterns for Vietnamese learners** 

 Some mistakes appear again and again because they come from differences between Vietnamese and English. If you know the pattern, you can catch the mistake more quickly. This chapter does not teach new grammar; it teaches awareness. Many learners plateau because they keep making the same ten or twenty errors. A practical writer studies personal error patterns and attacks them directly. 

 The most efficient approach is to keep a small table with three columns: my mistake, the correct pattern, and one new example from my life. The error must become visible before it can disappear. **Key principles** 

* Notice repeated missing be, article errors, and plural \-s errors.

## **31\. Pronunciation habits that affect grammar and vocabulary** 

 This book focuses on grammar and vocabulary, but speaking accuracy also depends on pronunciation habits. * Practise the three pronunciations of \-ed and \-s endings. 

* Learn word stress with vocabulary: imPORTant, deVELop, ecoNOmic, responSIBle. 

* In connected speech, strong grammar words can become weak: to, of, can, for, and. Listen actively for this.

 Phrasal verbs are common in speech and semi-formal writing. Some are transparent, but many have extended meanings that cannot be guessed from the individual words alone. Vietnamese learners often avoid them or use them too literally. You do not need to master hundreds at once, but you should learn the high-frequency ones that appear in work, study, and daily conversation. 

 A good practical approach is to group phrasal verbs by theme: communication, progress, problems, relationships, work, and organisation. **Practical principles** 

* Learn phrasal verbs with one clear meaning first and add other meanings later. 

* Notice separable and inseparable patterns: turn on the light/turn the light on; look after a child. 

* Use phrasal verbs naturally in speaking, but choose carefully in formal writing. 

* Many high-value phrasal verbs are common in meetings and study talk: find out, point out, carry out, work on, deal with, come up with.

## **37\. Formal vocabulary for writing and natural vocabulary for speaking** 

 Not every useful word belongs in every situation. Writing often needs neutral or formal vocabulary, while speaking often sounds better with shorter, more everyday choices. Strong learners recognise both options and choose based on context. The goal is not to avoid simple words; simple words are often the clearest. The goal is to know when you need something more formal and when you do not. 

 	This distinction is especially useful for Vietnamese learners preparing for work, study, presentations, or exams. You can keep the meaning the same while adjusting the tone. **Practical principles** 

* Formal writing often prefers verbs like reduce, increase, encourage, discourage, require, demonstrate, indicate, contribute to, and result in. 

* Natural speaking often prefers shorter everyday choices like cut down, go up, help, show, point out, bring about, and lead to. 

* Some formal words sound unnatural in casual speech if used too often. Some very casual words sound weak in writing. 

* Build pairs: ask/inquire, buy/purchase, help/assist, start/begin/commence, fix/resolve, get/obtain/receive.

## **38\. False friends and direct-translation traps for Vietnamese learners** 

 Some English errors are not grammar errors; they are translation traps. The sentence is understandable, but it is not natural English. These traps are especially common among Vietnamese learners because the Vietnamese expression feels logical. If you want practical improvement, this chapter may save you more time than learning twenty rare words. 

 Treat these items as memory pairs: wrong habit \-\> natural English. Review them often, because direct translation habits are stubborn. **Practical principles** 

* Do not translate every Vietnamese phrase word by word. 

* When an expression feels strange, check whether English uses a chunk instead of a literal translation. 

* Record traps that you personally use often. 

* Listen for natural alternatives in real conversations and podcasts. 

### **Vocabulary table** 

| Common trap  | Natural English  | Note  |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| open the light/close the light  | turn on the light/turn off the light  | English uses turn on/off.  |

| very delicious  | delicious/really good  | Delicious already carries strong meaning.  |
| learn knowledge  | gain 	knowledge/learn  something  | Knowledge is usually gained or acquired.  |
| discuss about  | discuss  | No about after discuss.  |

| make exercise  | do exercise/do exercises  | Do is the usual verb.  |
| one time/two times  | once/twice  | Use 	once/twice 	where possible.  |
| have free  | be free/have some free time  | Natural chunk differs.  |
| convenient for me  | works for me/is convenient for me  | Context matters.  |
| send for me  | send it to me  | Use 	natural 	preposition pattern.  |

## **Final advice** 

### **Keep this simple rule in mind** 

* Do not try to fix everything in one week. Consistent correction of repeated errors is more powerful than short periods of intense study. 

* When you learn new vocabulary, learn the pattern and the pronunciation at the same time. 

* Use English every day in small ways: short messages, mini summaries, voice notes, questions, and corrections. 

* The best learners do three things repeatedly: notice, use, and review.
